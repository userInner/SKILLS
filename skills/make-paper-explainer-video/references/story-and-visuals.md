# Story, visuals, and retention

## Choose one story engine

### Case-first - preferred when available

1. Specific person and artifact
2. Concrete decision or error
3. Why the context matters
4. A second contrasting or reinforcing case
5. The pattern both cases reveal
6. Study size and method
7. Main relationship or result
8. Correlation/causation and sampling boundary
9. Practical checking rule
10. Return to the opening risk

Strong opening:

> A participant nurse used ChatGPT to draft a patient handout, then checked it line by line against hospital guidance.

Weak opening:

> Researchers surveyed 319 knowledge workers about AI.

Label reported experiences accurately. The case creates attention; the study determines the claim.

### Misconception-first

1. Public claim
2. What the paper actually measured
3. Study design
4. Decisive result with denominator
5. Meaning of the measured construct
6. What it does not prove
7. Limitations
8. Practical conclusion

Use this when the correction itself is concrete and surprising.

### Experiment-first

Start with a visually understandable apparatus, task, or comparison. Reveal the result only after the viewer understands what participants did.

## Eight-second retention test

Before rendering, mute the opening and inspect its first frame, four-second frame, and eight-second frame.

The viewer should understand at least one of:

- who is making a decision;
- what could go wrong;
- what common belief is being challenged;
- what unusual experiment is happening.

If the opening only communicates a paper title, sample size, or generic “AI is changing everything,” rewrite it.

## Visual grammar

- Use warm gray desk or neutral page backgrounds.
- Preserve the paper's typography and figure colors.
- Use red for correction, warning, and focal comparison.
- Use yellow for one secondary emphasis or practical rule.
- Show one evidence object per shot.
- Crop case paragraphs until the relevant sentence is legible.
- Pair English evidence with a short Chinese interpretation, not a full translation block.
- Put captions below the evidence and above platform control zones.
- Use 2-4 second phrases instead of full narration transcripts.
- Reveal title page, venue, and sample after the hook when using a case-first structure.

Avoid glowing cards, animated neural networks, AI faces, fake terminals, generic office stock, decorative particles, and movement without informational purpose.

## Diagnose an unwatchable draft

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Feels like a lecture | Method and sample appear before stakes | Move a case, contradiction, or consequence to the first eight seconds |
| Feels empty | Conclusions lack methods, denominator, or error mode | Return to the paper and find the exact task or case |
| Feels dense | Too many numbers and qualifications arrive together | Keep one main relationship and move caveats immediately after it |
| Feels AI-generated | Generic stock, fake UI, symmetrical glowing cards | Use paper crops, real quotes, physical-document styling, and restrained annotations |
| Looks active but remains boring | Motion replaced narrative progression | Rewrite the first 20 seconds; do not add transitions |

## Ending patterns

End with a decision rule that a viewer can use immediately:

- “来源在哪里？适用于我的场景吗？如果错了，谁承担后果？”
- “先自己形成判断，再让 AI 找反例。”
- “保留第一轮思考，把 AI 放在验证和扩展阶段。”

Do not turn an untested practical implication into a claim that the paper proved the workflow works.
